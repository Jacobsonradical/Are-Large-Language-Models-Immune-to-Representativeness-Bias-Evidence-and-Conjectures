Your question is a great example of the base rate fallacy, where people tend to ignore the prior probability (or base rate) of an outcome in the presence of specific information.

In this case, while the description of Jason may seem to fit a stereotypical image of a computer science professor, we must consider the base rates - there are 75 computer science professors and 25 humanities professors in the sample.

So even before looking at the description, the probability of a randomly selected individual being a computer science professor is 75%, or 0.75.

The description of Jason is quite general and could apply to successful professors in many fields. It doesn't provide strong evidence to shift the probability significantly away from the base rate.

Therefore, my estimate for the probability that Jason is a computer science professor would be close to, but slightly lower than, the base rate of 0.75.
