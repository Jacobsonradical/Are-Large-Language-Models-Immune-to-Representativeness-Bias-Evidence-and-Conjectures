To calculate the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller given the witness's identification, we can use Bayes' Theorem.

Given:
- 85% of the population is shorter than 185 cm and 15% are 185 cm or taller.
- The witness correctly identifies a person's height 80% of the time and makes an error 20% of the time.

Let's denote:
- \(A\): The event that the attacker is 185 cm or taller.
- \(B\): The event that the witness identifies the attacker as being 185 cm or taller.

We need to calculate \(P(A|B)\), the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller given the witness's identification.

From Bayes' Theorem, we have:

\[ P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) * P(A)}{P(B)} \]

Where:
- \(P(A)\) is the prior probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller, which is 0.15.
- \(P(B|A)\) is the probability that the witness identifies a person as 185 cm or taller given that they are actually 185 cm or taller, which is 0.80.
- \(P(B)\) is the total probability that the witness identifies a person as 185 cm or taller, which can be calculated using the law of total probability:

\[ P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|\neg A) * P(\neg A) \]

Where:
- \(P(B|\neg A)\) is the probability that the witness identifies a person as 185 cm or taller given that they are actually shorter than 185 cm, which is 0.20 (since the witness makes an error 20% of the time).
- \(P(\neg A)\) is the probability that the attacker is shorter than 185 cm, which is 0.85.

Plugging in the values, we get:

\[ P(B) = (0.80 * 0.15) + (0.20 * 0.85) \]
\[ P(B) = 0.12 + 0.17 \]
\[ P(B) = 0.29 \]

Now, we can calculate \(P(A|B)\):

\[ P(A|B) = \frac{0.80 * 0.15}{0.29} \]
\[ P(A|B) = \frac{0.12}{0.29} \]
\[ P(A|B) \approx 0.4138 \]
