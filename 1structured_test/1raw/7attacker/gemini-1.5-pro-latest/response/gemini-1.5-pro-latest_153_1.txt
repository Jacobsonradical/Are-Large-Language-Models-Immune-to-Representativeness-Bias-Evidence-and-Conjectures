Here's how to approach this problem, which involves Bayesian reasoning:

**Understanding the Information**

* **Prior Probability:** The information about the city's population (85% shorter, 15% taller than 185cm) gives us the *prior probability* of a randomly selected person being 185cm or taller.
* **Witness Reliability (Likelihood):** The witness's 80% accuracy under similar conditions provides the *likelihood* of them correctly identifying someone's height.
* **Posterior Probability:** We want to find the *posterior probability* – the probability the attacker is 185cm or taller *given* the witness's statement.

**Applying Bayes' Theorem (simplified)**

While we can use Bayes' Theorem directly, a more intuitive approach is:

1. **Imagine 100 scenarios** where the witness makes an identification.
2. **Based on the city's population:** 15 out of these 100 scenarios would likely involve an attacker who is actually 185cm or taller, and 85 would involve someone shorter.
3. **Witness Accuracy:**
    * Out of the 15 scenarios with a taller attacker, the witness would correctly identify them 80% of the time (15 * 0.8 = 12).
    * Out of the 85 scenarios with a shorter attacker, the witness would incorrectly identify them as taller 20% of the time (85 * 0.2 = 17).
4. **Total "Tall" Identifications:** The witness would claim the attacker is tall in 29 cases (12 correct + 17 incorrect).
5. **Probability of Actually Being Tall:** Out of these 29 cases, only 12 involve a truly tall attacker. The probability is then 12/29 ≈ 0.41
