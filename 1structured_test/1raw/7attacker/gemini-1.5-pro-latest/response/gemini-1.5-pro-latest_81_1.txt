* **Population Distribution (b):** This information is crucial. It tells us the general likelihood of someone in the city being 185cm or taller.

**Applying Bayes' Theorem**

This problem is best tackled using Bayes' Theorem, which helps calculate conditional probability (the probability of an event happening given that another event has occurred).

Let:

* **Event A:** The attacker is 185cm or taller.
* **Event B:** The witness identifies the attacker as 185cm or taller.

We want to find **P(A|B)** - the probability the attacker is 185cm or taller *given* the witness said they were.

Bayes' Theorem states: 
  **P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)**

Let's break down each term:

* **P(B|A):** The probability the witness would identify the attacker as tall *given* the attacker is actually tall. This is 0.8 (80% accuracy).
* **P(A):** The prior probability of someone in the city being 185cm or taller. This is 0.15 (15% from the population data).
* **P(B):** The probability the witness would identify someone as tall (regardless of actual height).  This can be calculated using the law of total probability:
    * P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A)  +  P(B|~A) * P(~A) 
    * Where ~A means "not A" (attacker is shorter than 185cm)
    * P(B|~A) = 0.2 (witness incorrectly identifies a short person as tall 20% of the time)
    * P(~A) = 0.85 (85% of the population is shorter than 185cm)
