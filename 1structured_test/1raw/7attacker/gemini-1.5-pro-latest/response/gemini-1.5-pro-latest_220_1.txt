Here's how we can approach this problem:

**Understanding the Information**

* **Witness Reliability:** The witness is right 80% of the time about someone being 185cm or taller.  This doesn't directly tell us the attacker's height, just how often the witness is accurate.
* **Population Distribution:** This gives us the general likelihood of someone in the city being 185cm or taller.

**Combining the Information**

We need to combine the witness's accuracy with the population distribution to get a more accurate probability.  This is a classic example where Bayes' Theorem can be applied.

**Bayes' Theorem**

Bayes' Theorem gives us a way to update our belief about an event (attacker's height) based on new evidence (witness testimony).

Let:

*  A = Event that the attacker is 185cm or taller
*  B = Event that the witness identified the attacker as 185cm or taller

We want to find P(A|B), the probability the attacker is 185cm or taller *given* the witness said so.

Bayes' Theorem states:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Where:

* P(B|A) = Probability the witness would say "185cm or taller"  *if* the attacker actually was that height (this is 80% or 0.8).
* P(A) = Prior probability of someone in the city being 185cm or taller (this is 15% or 0.15).
* P(B) = Overall probability the witness would say "185cm or taller" (we'll calculate this below).

**Calculating P(B)**

P(B) has two components:

1.  Witness is right and the attacker is tall: P(B|A) * P(A) = 0.8 * 0.15 = 0.12
2.  Witness is wrong and the attacker is short: P(B|not A) * P(not A) = 0.2 * 0.85 = 0.17

So, P(B) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29

**Final Calculation**

Now we can plug everything into Bayes' Theorem:

P(A|B) = (0.8 * 0.15) / 0.29
