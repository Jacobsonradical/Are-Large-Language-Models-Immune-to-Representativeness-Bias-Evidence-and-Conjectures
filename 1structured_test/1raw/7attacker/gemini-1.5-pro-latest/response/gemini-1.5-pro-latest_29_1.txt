* **Population Data (b):** This gives us the base rate of people's heights in the city. It's more likely for a random person to be shorter than 185cm based on the population distribution.

**Combining the Information**

We need to combine the witness testimony with the population data to get a more accurate probability. This is a classic example where Bayesian reasoning is helpful.

**Let's break it down:**

* **Event A:** The attacker is 185cm or taller.
* **Event B:** The witness identified the attacker as 185cm or taller.

* **We want to find: P(A|B)** - The probability the attacker is actually 185cm or taller *given* the witness said they were.

**Here's the Bayesian formula in play:**

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Where:

* **P(B|A):** Probability the witness would say the attacker is 185cm+ *given* the attacker actually is 185cm+ (This is the witness's accuracy for true positives: 80% or 0.8).
* **P(A):** Prior probability of someone in the city being 185cm+ (This is the base rate from the population data: 15% or 0.15).
* **P(B):** Probability of the witness saying the attacker is 185cm+ (This can happen in two ways: the attacker is actually tall and the witness is right, OR the attacker is shorter and the witness is wrong).

**Calculating P(B):**
* P(B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] + [P(B|~A) * P(~A)]
   * P(B|~A) is the probability the witness says 185cm+ given the attacker is actually shorter (false positive rate, which is 20% or 0.2)
   * P(~A) is the probability someone in the city is shorter than 185cm (85% or 0.85)

* P(B) = (0.8 * 0.15) + (0.2 * 0.85) = 0.29

* P(A|B) = (0.8 * 0.15) / 0.29
