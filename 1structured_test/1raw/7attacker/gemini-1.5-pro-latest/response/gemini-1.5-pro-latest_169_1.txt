Here's how to approach this problem, breaking it down into parts:

**Understanding the Information**

* **Witness Reliability:** The witness is 80% accurate in judging height under similar conditions. This means:
    * If the attacker IS 185cm or taller, there's an 80% chance the witness is right.
    * If the attacker IS NOT 185cm or taller, there's a 20% chance the witness is wrong (and thus claiming they ARE taller).

* **Population Distribution:** This gives us the general likelihood of someone in the city being 185cm or taller, regardless of the attack.

**Applying Bayes' Theorem**

This problem is best tackled with Bayes' Theorem, which helps calculate conditional probabilities (the probability of something happening given that something else has already occurred).

Here's how it looks:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Where:

* **P(A|B):** The probability the attacker is 185cm or taller (A) GIVEN that the witness said so (B). This is what we want to find. 
* **P(B|A):** The probability the witness would say the attacker is 185cm or taller GIVEN that the attacker actually IS 185cm or taller. This is 0.8 (80%).
* **P(A):** The prior probability of someone in the city being 185cm or taller. This is 0.15 (15%).
* **P(B):** The probability the witness would say someone is 185cm or taller (regardless of whether they actually are). This needs to be calculated.

**Calculating P(B)**

P(B) is the probability the witness says "185cm or taller."  There are two ways this could happen:

1. **The attacker IS tall, and the witness is right:** P(attacker is tall) * P(witness correct | attacker is tall) = 0.15 * 0.8 = 0.12
2. **The attacker IS NOT tall, and the witness is wrong:**  P(attacker is not tall) * P(witness wrong | attacker is not tall) = 0.85 * 0.2 = 0.17

P(B) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29

**Putting it Together**

Now we have all the pieces for Bayes' Theorem:
