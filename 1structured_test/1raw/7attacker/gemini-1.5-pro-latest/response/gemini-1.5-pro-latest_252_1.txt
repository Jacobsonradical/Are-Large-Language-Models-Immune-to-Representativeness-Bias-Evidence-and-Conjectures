**Understanding the Problem**

**Using Bayes' Theorem (optional but helpful for understanding)**

Bayes' Theorem provides a framework for updating our belief based on new evidence:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Where:

* P(A|B):  Probability of the attacker being 185cm+  (A) *given* the witness said so (B) - what we want to find.
* P(B|A):  Probability the witness would say "185cm+" *if* the attacker truly was 185cm+ (80% accuracy).
* P(A):     Prior probability of someone in the city being 185cm+ (15%).
* P(B):     Probability of the witness saying "185cm+" (this is more complex to calculate directly).

**Calculating the Estimate**

While we could calculate P(B) for a precise Bayes' Theorem solution, a reasonable estimate can be made:

* **The witness's testimony significantly increases the likelihood.** They are more likely to say "185cm+" if the attacker is actually that height.
* **However, their 20% error rate means we can't be certain.** There's still a chance they misjudged.

Considering this, the probability of the attacker being 185cm or taller is now significantly higher than the initial 15% but not as high as the witness's 80% accuracy.  A reasonable estimate would lie somewhere between these two values.
