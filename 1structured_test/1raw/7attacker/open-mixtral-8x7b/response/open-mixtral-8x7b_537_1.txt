To solve this problem, we can use Bayes' theorem which allows us to update our prior beliefs about the probability of an event based on evidence.

First, let's denote:
- H: The event that the attacker's height is 185 cm or taller.
- E: The event that the witness identified the attacker as being 185 cm or taller.

We want to find P(H|E), the probability that the attacker's height was 185 cm or taller given that a witness identified the attacker as being 185 cm or taller.

According to Bayes' theorem:

P(H|E) = P(E|H) * P(H) / P(E)

1. P(E|H) is the probability of the witness identifying the attacker as 185 cm or taller given that the attacker is indeed 185 cm or taller. Since the witness correctly identifies a person's height 80% of the time when they are actually 185 cm or taller, P(E|H) = 0.8.

2. P(H) is the probability of the attacker being 185 cm or taller. According to the given data, 15% of the population is 185 cm or taller, so P(H) = 0.15.

3. P(E) is the probability of the witness identifying the attacker as 185 cm or taller. We can calculate it as the sum of probabilities of identifying a person as 185 cm or taller given they are, or they are not:

P(E) = P(E|H) * P(H) + P(E|not H) * P(not H)

We already know P(E|H) = 0.8 and P(H) = 0.15.
P(not H) = 1 - P(H) = 0.85

To calculate P(E|not H), it's the probability of the witness identifying the attacker as 185 cm or taller given that the attacker is not (1 - 0.15 = 0.85). The witness errs 20% of the time when identifying someone shorter, so P(E|not H) = 0.2

Now we can calculate P(E):
P(E) = 0.8 * 0.15 + 0.2 * 0.85 = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29
