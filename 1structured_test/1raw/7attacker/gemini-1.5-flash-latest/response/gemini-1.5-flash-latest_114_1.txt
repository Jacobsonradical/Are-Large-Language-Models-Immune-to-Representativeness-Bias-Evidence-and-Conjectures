Bayes' Theorem helps us update our belief about an event (attacker's height) based on new evidence (witness testimony). 

Let's define events:

* **A:** The attacker's height is 185 cm or taller.
* **B:** The witness identifies the attacker as 185 cm or taller.

We want to find P(A|B) - the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller given the witness's identification.

Bayes' Theorem states:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

* **P(B|A):** The probability of the witness identifying someone as 185 cm or taller given they *actually* are 185 cm or taller. This is the witness's accuracy, which is 80% or 0.8.

    * P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|not A) * P(not A)

    * P(not A) = 0.85 (the probability of someone being shorter than 185 cm)
    * P(B) = (0.8 * 0.15) + (0.2 * 0.85) = 0.29
