**Understanding the Information**

* **Information a:**  Tells us the general height distribution in the city. 15% of the population is 185 cm or taller.
* **Information b:**  Gives us the witness's accuracy in identifying height, which is important because we don't have direct height information from the victim.

**Applying Bayes' Theorem**

Bayes' Theorem is a powerful tool for updating our beliefs based on new evidence.  Let's define:

* **Event A:** The attacker is 185 cm or taller.
* **Event B:** The witness identified the attacker as being 185 cm or taller.

We want to find P(A|B), the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller *given* that the witness identified them as such.  Bayes' Theorem states:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Let's break down each term:

* **P(B|A):** The probability the witness *correctly* identifies someone as 185 cm or taller, given they actually are. This is the witness's accuracy, 80%.
* **P(A):** The prior probability of someone being 185 cm or taller in the city, which is 15% (from information a).
* **P(B):** The probability of the witness identifying someone as 185 cm or taller. This requires considering both correct identifications and incorrect ones. We can use the law of total probability:
    *  P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|not A) * P(not A) 
        * P(B|not A) is the probability of the witness incorrectly identifying someone as 185 cm or taller (20%).
        * P(not A) is the probability of someone being shorter than 185 cm (85%).

**Calculation**

1. **P(B):** 
   * P(B) = (0.80 * 0.15) + (0.20 * 0.85) = 0.29
2. **P(A|B):**
   * P(A|B) = (0.80 * 0.15) / 0.29 = 0.414 

**Answer:**

{probability: 0.414}
