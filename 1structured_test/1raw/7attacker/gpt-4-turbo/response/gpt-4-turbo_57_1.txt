To solve this problem, we make use of Bayes' theorem, which allows us to update our prior belief about a hypothesis based on new evidence. In this case, the hypothesis is that the attacker was 185 cm or taller (let's denote this hypothesis as \( H \)), and the evidence is that the witness identified the attacker as 185 cm or taller (let's denote this as \( E \)).

From Bayes' theorem, the posterior probability \( P(H \mid E) \) (the probability the attacker was 185 cm or taller given that the witness identified them as such) can be calculated as:

\[
P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)}
\]
where 
- \( P(H) \) is the prior probability that the attacker was 185 cm or taller, which is 0.15 (15%).
- \( P(E \mid H) \) is the probability of the witness correctly identifying the attacker as 185 cm or taller given that they were indeed 185 cm or taller, which is 0.8 (80%).
- \( P(E) \) is the total probability of the witness identifying someone as 185 cm or taller (regardless of their actual height).

\( P(E) \) can be calculated using the law of total probability:

\[
P(E) = P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H) + P(E \mid H^c) \cdot P(H^c)
\]
where \( H^c \) is the complement of hypothesis \( H \) (the attacker was shorter than 185 cm), and:
- \( P(H^c) \) is 0.85 (85% are shorter).
- \( P(E \mid H^c) \) is the error rate, or the probability of falsely identifying someone as taller when they are not 185 cm or taller, which is 0.2 (20%).

Thus:

\[
P(E) = (0.8 \times 0.15) + (0.2 \times 0.85) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29
\]

Substituting these values back into Bayes' theorem:

\[
P(H \mid E) = \frac{0.8 \times 0.15}{0.29} = \frac{0.12}{0.29} \approx 0.4138
\]

Therefore, the probability that the attacker was 185 cm or taller, given the witness's identification, is approximately 0.4138.
