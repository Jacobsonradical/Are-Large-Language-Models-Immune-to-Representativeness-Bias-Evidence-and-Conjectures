Here's how to approach this problem using Bayesian reasoning:

**Let's define some events:**

* **A:** The attacker's height was actually 185 cm or taller.
* **B:** The first witness claimed the attacker was 185 cm or taller.

**We want to find P(A|B), the probability the attacker was actually tall given the witness's testimony.**

**We can use Bayes' Theorem:**

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Let's break down each term:

* **P(B|A):** The probability the witness would claim the attacker was tall *given* the attacker was actually tall. This is the witness's accuracy rate for tall people, which is 80% or 0.8.

* **P(A):** The prior probability of the attacker being tall. We can use the city's population distribution for this: 15% or 0.15.

* **P(B):** The probability of the witness claiming the attacker was tall (regardless of the actual height).  This can be calculated using the law of total probability:
    * P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|~A) * P(~A) 
    * Where ~A means "attacker is not tall"
    * P(B|~A) is the probability the witness would claim "tall" when the attacker is short (their error rate for short people), which is 20% or 0.2.
    * P(~A) is the probability of a person in the city being shorter than 185cm, which is 85% or 0.85.

   * P(B) = (0.8 * 0.15) + (0.2 * 0.85) = 0.29
