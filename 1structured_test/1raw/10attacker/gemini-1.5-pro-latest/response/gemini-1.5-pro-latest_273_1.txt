Here's how we can approach this problem using Bayesian reasoning:

**Let's define some events:**

* **A:** The attacker is 185 cm or taller.
* **B:** The attacker is shorter than 185 cm.
* **W1:** The first witness claims the attacker is 185 cm or taller.
* **W2:** The second witness claims the attacker is shorter than 185 cm.

**We are given the following probabilities:**

* **P(A) = 0.15** (Prior probability of attacker being 185 cm or taller)
* **P(B) = 0.85** (Prior probability of attacker being shorter than 185 cm)
* **P(W1|A) = 0.8** (Probability witness 1 correctly identifies someone 185cm or taller)
* **P(W1|B) = 0.2** (Probability witness 1 incorrectly identifies someone shorter as 185cm or taller)
* **P(W2|A) = 0.3** (Probability witness 2 incorrectly identifies someone 185cm or taller as shorter)
* **P(W2|B) = 0.7** (Probability witness 2 correctly identifies someone shorter than 185cm) 

**We want to find P(A|W1, W2), the probability the attacker is 185cm or taller given both witness statements.**

Since it's difficult to directly calculate  P(A|W1, W2), we'll use Bayes' Theorem and focus on finding P(W1, W2 | A) and P(W1, W2 | B) instead. 

**Assuming the witnesses' judgments are independent:**

* **P(W1, W2 | A) = P(W1|A) * P(W2|A) = 0.8 * 0.3 = 0.24**
* **P(W1, W2 | B) = P(W1|B) * P(W2|B) = 0.2 * 0.7 = 0.14**

**Now we can apply Bayes' Theorem:**

P(A|W1, W2) = [P(W1, W2|A) * P(A)] / P(W1, W2)

To find P(W1, W2), we use the law of total probability:

P(W1, W2) = P(W1, W2|A) * P(A) + P(W1, W2|B) * P(B) 
              = (0.24 * 0.15) + (0.14 * 0.85) 
              = 0.155

**Finally, we can calculate:**

P(A|W1, W2) = (0.24 * 0.15) / 0.155 â‰ˆ 0.232
