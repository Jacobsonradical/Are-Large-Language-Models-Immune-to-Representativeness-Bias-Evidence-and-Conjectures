To calculate the probability that the attacker was 185 cm tall or taller, given the witness considerations, we use Bayes' theorem. Bayes' theorem is a way to update original beliefs (prior probabilities) with new evidence (likelihoods provided by the witnesses).

Let's define:
- \( H \) as the hypothesis that the attacker is 185 cm or taller.
- \( \neg H \) as the hypothesis that the attacker is shorter than 185 cm.

From the information:
- Prior probabilities based on population data: \( P(H) = 0.15 \) (15%) and \( P(\neg H) = 0.85 \) (85%).
- The first witness accuracy, \( W_1 \): 
   - \( P(W_1 | H) = 0.8 \) (80% chance the witness correctly identifies someone 185 cm or taller)
   - \( P(W_1 | \neg H) = 0.2 \) (20% chance the witness incorrectly says the attacker is 185 cm or taller)
- The second witness accuracy, \( W_2 \):
   - \( P(W_2 | H) = 0.3 \) (30% chance the witness incorrectly says the attacker is shorter)
   - \( P(W_2 | \neg H) = 0.7 \) (70% chance the witness correctly says they're shorter)

Bayes' theorem states:
\[ P(H | W_1 \cap W_2) = \frac{P(W_1 \cap W_2 | H) \cdot P(H)}{P(W_1 \cap W_2)} \]

Where:
- \( P(W_1 \cap W_2 | H) = P(W_1 | H) \cdot P(W_2 | H) \) (assuming independence of witness statements given the height)
- \( P(W_1 \cap W_2 | \neg H) = P(W_1 | \neg H) \cdot P(W_2 | \neg H) \)
- The total probability of both witnesses' statements, \( P(W_1 \cap W_2) \), is:
  \[ P(W_1 \cap W_2) = P(W_1 \cap W_2 | H) \cdot P(H) + P(W_1 \cap W_2 | \neg H) \cdot P(\neg H) \]

Calculations:
- \( P(W_1 \cap W_2 | H) = 0.8 \times 0.3 = 0.24 \)
- \( P(W_1 \cap W_2 | \neg H) = 0.2 \times 0.7 = 0.14 \)
- \( P(W_1 \cap W_2) = (0.24 \times 0.15) + (0.14 \times 0.85) = 0.036 + 0.119 = 0.155 \)

Therefore:
\[ P(H | W_1 \cap W_2) = \frac{0.24 \times 0.15}{0.155} = \frac{0.036}{0.155} \approx 0.2323 \]
