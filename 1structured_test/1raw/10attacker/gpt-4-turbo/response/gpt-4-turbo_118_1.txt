To determine the probability that the attacker was 185 cm or taller given the evidence and witness testimonies, we will use Bayes' theorem. Bayes' theorem allows us to update our prior beliefs based on new evidence.

Let's denote:
- \( H \) as the hypothesis that the attacker's height is 185 cm or taller.
- \( H^c \) as the complementary hypothesis that the attacker is shorter than 185 cm.
  
We are provided with the following information:
- \( P(H) = 0.15 \) (15% of the population is 185 cm or taller).
- \( P(H^c) = 0.85 \) (85% of the population is shorter than 185 cm).

We also have the accuracy of the witnesses:
- Witness 1, who said the attacker is 185 cm or taller, is correct 80% of the time.
  - \( P(\text{W1}|H) = 0.8 \) (probability Witness 1 reports ≥185 cm, given the attacker is ≥185 cm)
  - \( P(\text{W1}|H^c) = 0.2 \) (probability Witness 1 reports ≥185 cm, given the attacker is <185 cm)
- Witness 2, who said the attacker is shorter than 185 cm, is correct 70% of the time.
  - \( P(\text{W2}|H) = 0.3 \) (probability Witness 2 reports <185 cm, given the attacker is ≥185 cm)
  - \( P(\text{W2}|H^c) = 0.7 \) (probability Witness 2 reports <185 cm, given the attacker is <185 cm)

To find \( P(H|\text{W1}) \) (the probability that the attacker is ≥185 cm given Witness 1's claim), we use Bayes' theorem:

\[
P(H|\text{W1}) = \frac{P(\text{W1}|H) P(H)}{P(\text{W1})}
\]

Where:
\[
P(\text{W1}) = P(\text{W1}|H) P(H) + P(\text{W1}|H^c) P(H^c) = (0.8 \times 0.15) + (0.2 \times 0.85) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29
\]

Thus, we get:
\[
P(H|\text{W1}) = \frac{0.12}{0.29} \approx 0.4138
\]

Since there is conflicting testimony between Witness 1 and Witness 2, and Witness 1 with an accuracy of 80%, which suggests high reliability, we have that \( P(H|\text{W1}) \approx 0.4138 \).
