To solve this problem, we need to use Bayes' theorem, which allows us to update the prior probability of an event based on new evidence.

Given information:
- The prior probability of the attacker being 185 cm or taller is 0.15 (from "b").
- The witness correctly identifies a person's height 80% of the time when the person is 185 cm or taller (true positive rate).
- The witness incorrectly identifies a person's height 20% of the time when the person is shorter than 185 cm (false positive rate).

Let's define the events:
A: The attacker is 185 cm or taller.
B: The witness identifies the attacker as 185 cm or taller.

We want to find P(A|B), the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller, given that the witness identified them as such.

Using Bayes' theorem:
P(A|B) = (P(B|A) * P(A)) / P(B)

P(B|A) is the true positive rate, which is 0.8 (from "a").
P(A) is the prior probability of the attacker being 185 cm or taller, which is 0.15 (from "b").

To find P(B), we need to use the law of total probability:
P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|¬A) * P(¬A)

P(B|¬A) is the false positive rate, which is 0.2 (from "a").
P(¬A) is the prior probability of the attacker being shorter than 185 cm, which is 0.85 (1 - 0.15).

Substituting the values:
P(B) = (0.8 * 0.15) + (0.2 * 0.85) = 0.29

Finally, we can calculate P(A|B):
P(A|B) = (0.8 * 0.15) / 0.29 ≈ 0.414
