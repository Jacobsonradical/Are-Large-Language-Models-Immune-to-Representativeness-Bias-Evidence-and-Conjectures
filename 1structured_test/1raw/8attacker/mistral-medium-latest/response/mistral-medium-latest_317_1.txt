To solve this problem, we can use Bayes' theorem. Let's denote:

P(A) as the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller (the prior probability)
P(B|A) as the probability that the witness identifies the attacker as 185 cm or taller given that the attacker is 185 cm or taller (the likelihood of a true positive)
P(B|¬A) as the probability that the witness identifies the attacker as 185 cm or taller given that the attacker is shorter than 185 cm (the likelihood of a false positive)
P(B) as the probability that the witness identifies the attacker as 185 cm or taller

From the given information:
P(B|A) = 0.8 (the witness correctly identifies height 80% of the time)
P(B|¬A) = 0.2 (the witness errs in 20% of the cases)
P(A) = 0.5 (the population of people shorter than 185 cm and those 185 cm or taller is roughly equal in size)
P(¬A) = 1 - P(A) = 0.5

However, we also have information about the distribution of attackers:
P(attacker | A) = 0.15 (15% attackers are 185 cm or taller)
P(attacker | ¬A) = 0.85 (85% attackers are under 185 cm)

We want to find the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller given that the witness identified the attacker as 185 cm or taller: P(A|B). Using Bayes' theorem, we have:

P(A|B) = P(B|A) \* P(A) / [P(B|A) \* P(A) + P(B|¬A) \* P(¬A)]

But we need to find P(B) first, which can be calculated using the law of total probability:

P(B) = P(B|A) \* P(A) + P(B|¬A) \* P(¬A)

Since we have information about the distribution of attackers, we should modify the above equation as follows:

P(B) = P(B|A) \* P(attacker | A) \* P(A) + P(B|¬A) \* P(attacker | ¬A) \* P(¬A)

Now we can calculate P(B):
P(B) = (0.8 \* 0.15 \* 0.5) + (0.2 \* 0.85 \* 0.5) ≈ 0.18 + 0.085 ≈ 0.265

Now we can calculate P(A|B):
P(A|B) = (0.8 \* 0.15 \* 0.5) / 0.265 ≈ 0.06 / 0.265 ≈ 0.2264
