Here's how to break down this probability problem:

**Understanding the Information**

* **Witness Reliability (a):** The witness is 80% accurate in judging height in similar conditions. This means they are right 80% of the time, but wrong 20% of the time.
* **Attacker Base Rates (b):** This tells us how likely an attacker is to be a certain height *regardless* of the witness testimony.  85% of attackers are under 185cm, and 15% are 185cm or taller.

**The Key: We Need Bayes' Theorem**

Bayes' Theorem helps us calculate probability when we have prior knowledge (base rates) and new evidence (witness testimony).

**Let's Define Events**

* **Event A:** The attacker is 185cm or taller.
* **Event B:** The witness identifies the attacker as 185cm or taller.

**We Want to Find:** P(A|B) - The probability the attacker is actually 185cm or taller *given* the witness said they were.

**Bayes' Theorem Formula**

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Where:

* **P(B|A):** Probability the witness would say the attacker is tall *given* the attacker is actually tall (80% or 0.8).
* **P(A):** Prior probability of an attacker being tall (15% or 0.15).
* **P(B):** Probability of the witness saying the attacker is tall (This has two components, we'll calculate below).

**Calculating P(B)**

The witness could say "tall" in two ways:

1. **Correctly:** The attacker is tall, and they identify them correctly (0.15 * 0.8 = 0.12)
2. **Incorrectly:** The attacker is short, but they misidentify them as tall (0.85 * 0.2 = 0.17)

Therefore: P(B) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29

**Putting it All Together**

P(A|B) = (0.8 * 0.15) / 0.29
