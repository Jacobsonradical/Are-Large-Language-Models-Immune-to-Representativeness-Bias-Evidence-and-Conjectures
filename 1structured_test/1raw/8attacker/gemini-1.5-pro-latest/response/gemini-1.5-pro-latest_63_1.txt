Here's how to break down this probability problem:

**Understanding the Information**

* **Attacker Demographics (b):**  This tells us about the general pool of potential attackers:
    * 85% are under 185cm
    * 15% are 185cm or taller

**The Key: We Need to Combine Both Pieces of Information**

It's tempting to say there's a 15% chance the attacker is tall based on (b). However, the witness testimony (even though imperfect) provides additional evidence.

**Think of it this way:**

1. **Scenario 1: The attacker IS 185cm or taller.** 
   * There's an 80% chance the witness correctly identified them.

2. **Scenario 2: The attacker IS SHORTER than 185cm.**
   * There's a 20% chance the witness *incorrectly* said they were 185cm or taller.

**To get a more accurate probability, we need to use Bayes' Theorem. It's a way to update our beliefs based on new evidence.**

**Unfortunately, we don't have enough information for a precise Bayes' calculation.**  We would need to know things like:

* How many people in the witness test were *actually* 185cm or taller (not just the split in the sample).
* Whether the witness tends to overestimate or underestimate height.

**Making an Informed Estimate**

Given the limitations, here's how we can make a reasonable estimate:

1. **The witness testimony increases the likelihood** that the attacker is tall (since they said so with 80% accuracy).
2. **However, tall attackers are still less common overall** (only 15% of attackers).

Therefore, the probability is likely higher than 15% (the base rate) but lower than 80% (the witness's accuracy).
