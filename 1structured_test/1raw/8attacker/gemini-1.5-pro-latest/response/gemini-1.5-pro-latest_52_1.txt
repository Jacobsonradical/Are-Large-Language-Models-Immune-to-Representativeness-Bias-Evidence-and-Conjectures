Here's how to break down this probability problem:

**Understanding the Information**

* **Witness Reliability (a):** The witness is right about height 80% of the time. This doesn't directly tell us the attacker's height, but it gives us a measure of the witness's testimony accuracy.
* **Attacker Demographics (b):** This is crucial.  It tells us that attackers who are 185cm or taller are much less common (15%) compared to shorter attackers (85%).

**Applying Bayes' Theorem**

This problem is best solved using Bayes' Theorem, which helps us calculate probabilities with new evidence:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Let's define our events:

* **Event A:** The attacker is 185cm or taller.
* **Event B:** The witness identified the attacker as 185cm or taller.

We want to find **P(A|B)** - the probability the attacker is actually tall *given* the witness said they were.

Here's how we break it down:

* **P(B|A):** The probability the witness would say "tall" IF the attacker was actually tall. This is the witness's reliability when the attacker IS tall (80% or 0.8). 
* **P(A):** The prior probability of an attacker being tall. This is the demographic information, which is low (15% or 0.15).
* **P(B):** The probability the witness would say "tall" regardless of the attacker's actual height. This needs further calculation:

    * The witness could say "tall" correctly when the attacker IS tall [P(B|A) * P(A) = 0.8 * 0.15 = 0.12]
    * The witness could say "tall" incorrectly when the attacker is SHORT [P(B|not A) * P(not A) = 0.2 * 0.85 = 0.17]
    * P(B) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29

**Calculation**

Now we have everything for Bayes' Theorem:

P(A|B) = (0.8 * 0.15) / 0.29
