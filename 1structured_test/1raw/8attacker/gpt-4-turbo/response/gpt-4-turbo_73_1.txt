To estimate the probability that the attacker's height was 185 cm or taller given the information provided, we need to use Bayes' Theorem. This theorem helps update the probability estimate for a hypothesis based on new evidence.

Let:
- \( H \) be the hypothesis that the attacker's height is 185 cm or taller.
- \( E \) be the evidence that the witness identified the attacker as being 185 cm or taller.

From the problem, we know:
- The true positive rate (sensitivity): The probability that the witness correctly identified someone as 185 cm or taller given they are actually 185 cm or taller is 80% or 0.8, thus \( P(E|H) = 0.8 \).
- The false positive rate: The probability that the witness incorrectly identified someone as 185 cm or taller when they are actually shorter than 185 cm is 20% or 0.2, thus \( P(E|H^c) = 0.2 \).
- The prior probability that a random attacker is 185 cm or taller, \( P(H) \), based on the distribution of attackers, is given as 15% of attackers being tall (185 cm and taller) and 85% being shorter. Hence \( P(H) = 0.15 \) and \( P(H^c) = 0.85 \).

Plugging into Bayes' Theorem:
\[ P(H|E) = \frac{P(E|H) \times P(H)}{P(E)} \]

First, we find \( P(E) \) using the Law of Total Probability:
\[ P(E) = P(E|H) \times P(H) + P(E|H^c) \times P(H^c) \]
\[ P(E) = (0.8 \times 0.15) + (0.2 \times 0.85) \]
\[ P(E) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29 \]

Now, substituting back into Bayes' formula:
\[ P(H|E) = \frac{0.8 \times 0.15}{0.29} \]
\[ P(H|E) = \frac{0.12}{0.29} \approx 0.4138 \]

Thus, the probability that the attacker's height was 185 cm or taller, given the witness's identification, is approximately 0.4138.
