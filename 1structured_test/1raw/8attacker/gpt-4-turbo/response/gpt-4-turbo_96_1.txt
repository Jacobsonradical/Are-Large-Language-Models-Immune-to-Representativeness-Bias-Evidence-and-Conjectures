To solve this problem, we can apply Bayes' Theorem. This will help us combine the given data on the distribution of attackers' height in the population with the accuracy of the witness's identification.

Let's define:
- \( H \) as the event the attacker is 185 cm or taller.
- \( W \) as the witness identifies the attacker as 185 cm or taller.

We know from the information given:
- \( P(H) \), the prior probability that a random attacker is 185 cm or taller, is 0.15 (since 15% of attackers are 185 cm or taller).
- \( P(\bar{H}) \), the prior probability that a random attacker is under 185 cm, is 0.85.
- \( P(W|H) \), the probability that the witness correctly identifies an attacker as 185 cm or taller given that they are indeed that tall, is 0.80.
- \( P(W|\bar{H}) \), the probability that the witness mistakenly identifies an attacker as 185 cm or taller when they are not, is 0.20.

We want to find \( P(H|W) \), the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller given that the witness said so.

According to Bayes' Theorem:
\[ P(H|W) = \frac{P(W|H) P(H)}{P(W)} \]
Where 
\[ P(W) = P(W|H)P(H) + P(W|\bar{H})P(\bar{H}) \]

Plugging in the values:
\[ P(W) = (0.80 \times 0.15) + (0.20 \times 0.85) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29 \]

So,
\[ P(H|W) = \frac{0.80 \times 0.15}{0.29} = \frac{0.12}{0.29} â‰ˆ 0.4138 \]

Thus, the estimated probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller, given the witness identification, is approximately 0.4138.
