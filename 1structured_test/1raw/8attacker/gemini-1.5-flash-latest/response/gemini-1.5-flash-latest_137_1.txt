**Applying Bayes' Theorem**

Bayes' Theorem helps us update our belief about an event (attacker's height) based on new evidence (the witness's testimony).

Let's define:

* **A:** Event that the attacker is 185 cm or taller.
* **B:** Event that the witness identifies the attacker as 185 cm or taller.

We want to find P(A|B), the probability that the attacker is 185 cm or taller given the witness's testimony.

Bayes' Theorem states:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Let's break down each term:

* **P(B|A):** Probability of the witness identifying the attacker as 185 cm or taller given the attacker is actually 185 cm or taller (this is the witness's accuracy). This is 0.8 (80%).
* **P(A):** Prior probability of the attacker being 185 cm or taller (this is the overall prevalence of tall attackers). This is 0.15 (15%).
* **P(B):**  Probability of the witness identifying the attacker as 185 cm or taller (regardless of the actual height). This is a bit trickier and requires us to consider both scenarios:

    * **Scenario 1:** Attacker is actually tall (185 cm or taller): P(B|A) * P(A) = 0.8 * 0.15 = 0.12
    * **Scenario 2:** Attacker is actually short (under 185 cm): P(B|not A) * P(not A) = 0.2 * 0.85 = 0.17

    We add these probabilities to get the overall P(B) = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29
