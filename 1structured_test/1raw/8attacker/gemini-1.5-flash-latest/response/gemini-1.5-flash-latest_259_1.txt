**Applying Bayes' Theorem**

Bayes' Theorem helps us update our belief about an event (the attacker being tall) based on new evidence (the witness's testimony).

Let's define:

* **A:** The event that the attacker is 185 cm or taller.
* **B:** The event that the witness identifies the attacker as being 185 cm or taller.

We want to find P(A|B), the probability that the attacker is tall given the witness's testimony. 

Bayes' Theorem states:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Let's break down each term:

* **P(B|A):** The probability the witness identifies the attacker as tall given they are actually tall. This is the witness's accuracy rate: 0.80.
* **P(A):** The prior probability that the attacker is tall. This is the base rate from information b: 0.15.
* **P(B):** The probability the witness identifies the attacker as tall (regardless of their actual height). This requires a bit more calculation.

**Calculating P(B)**

We can use the law of total probability:

P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|not A) * P(not A)

* **P(B|not A):** The probability the witness identifies the attacker as tall given they are actually short. This is the witness's error rate: 0.20.
* **P(not A):** The prior probability the attacker is not tall (shorter than 185 cm): 0.85.

P(B) = (0.80 * 0.15) + (0.20 * 0.85) = 0.29
