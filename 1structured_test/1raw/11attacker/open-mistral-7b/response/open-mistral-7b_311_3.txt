Bayes' theorem states that the probability of an event A given another event B is proportional to the probability of event B given A times the prior probability of A. In this case, A is the event "attacker is black," and B is the event "witness identifies attacker as black."

- P(A) as the prior probability of the attacker being black (given in the first piece of information: 15% or 0.15)
- P(B|A) as the likelihood of the witness correctly identifying the attacker as black given that the attacker is black (given in the second piece of information: 80% or 0.8)
- P(B|~A) as the likelihood of the witness incorrectly identifying the attacker as black given that the attacker is not black (given in the second piece of information: 20% or 0.2)
- P(~A) as the prior probability of the attacker not being black (1 - P(A))

We are asked to find P(A|B), the probability of the attacker being black given that the witness identified the attacker as black.

Using Bayes' theorem:

P(A|B) = P(B|A) * P(A) / [P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|~A) * P(~A)]
