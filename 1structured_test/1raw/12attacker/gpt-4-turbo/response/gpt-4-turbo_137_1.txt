To estimate the probability that the attacker is male given the provided pieces of information, we can use Bayes' theorem, which in this context relates the probability of the attacker being male given the witness's identification to the overall probabilities of the attacker's gender and the reliability of the witness.

Let \( M \) represent the event that the attacker is male, and \( W_M \) represent the event that the witness identifies the attacker as male. We want to find \( P(M | W_M) \) – the probability that the attacker is male given that the witness identified the attacker as male.

We use Bayes' theorem, which states:
\[
P(M | W_M) = \frac{P(W_M | M) \times P(M)}{P(W_M)}
\]

Where:
- \( P(M) = 0.15 \) is the prior probability that the attacker is male, given no other information (15% of population is male).
- \( P(W_M | M) \) is the probability that the witness identifies the attacker as male given that the attacker is indeed male. From the problem, this is 80% (witness correctly identifies gender 80% of the time).
- \( P(W_M) \) is the total probability of the witness identifying the attacker as male. This can be broken down using the law of total probability:
  \[
  P(W_M) = P(W_M | M) \times P(M) + P(W_M | F) \times P(F)
  \]
  where \( P(F) = 0.85 \) is the probability that the attacker is female, and \( P(W_M | F) \) is the probability that the witness incorrectly identifies a female as male, which is 20% (since witness wrongly identifies gender 20% of the time).

Now, calculate \( P(W_M) \):
\[
P(W_M) = 0.8 \times 0.15 + 0.2 \times 0.85 = 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29
\]

Substitute these values back into Bayes’ theorem:
\[
P(M | W_M) = \frac{0.8 \times 0.15}{0.29} \approx \frac{0.12}{0.29} \approx 0.4138
\]

Therefore, \( P(M | W_M) \approx 0.41 \). This reflects that, even though the population has more females, the evidence from the witness substantially increases the probability that the attacker is male.
