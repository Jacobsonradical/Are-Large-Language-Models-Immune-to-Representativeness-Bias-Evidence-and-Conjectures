To solve this problem, we can use Bayes' theorem which relates the conditional and marginal probabilities of two events. Let's denote:

A - the event that the attacker is male,
B - the event that the witness identified the attacker as male.

We are asked to find P(A|B), the probability that the attacker is male given that the witness identified the attacker as male.

According to Bayes' theorem, P(A|B) = P(B|A) * P(A) / P(B)

From the given information, we know that P(A) = 0.15 (15% of the population is male), and P(B|A) = 0.8 (the witness correctly identifies a male as male 80% of the time).

However, we don't have enough information to directly calculate P(B). Instead, we can calculate P(B|A) and P(B|not A), and then use the law of total probability to calculate P(B).

P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|not A) * P(not A)
= (0.8)(0.15) + P(B|not A) * (1 - 0.15)

P(B|not A) = 0.2 (the witness incorrectly identifies a female as male 20% of the time)
