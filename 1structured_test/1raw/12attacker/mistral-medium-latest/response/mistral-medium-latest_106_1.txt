To solve this problem, we can use Bayes' theorem, which is a formula that helps us find the probability of a hypothesis (in this case, the attacker being male) given some evidence.

First, let's define our variables:
H: The hypothesis that the attacker is male.
E: The evidence that the witness identified the attacker as male.
P(H): The prior probability of the attacker being male, which is 0.15 (from information a).
P(E|H): The likelihood of the evidence given that the attacker is male, which is 1.0 since the witness always correctly identifies males as males 80% of the time and has no reason to misidentify them as females when they are indeed males.
P(E|¬H): The likelihood of the evidence given that the attacker is not male, which is 0.2 (from information b, as the witness incorrectly identifies females as males 20% of the time).
P(¬H): The prior probability of the attacker not being male, which is 0.85 (from information a).

Now, we can apply Bayes' theorem:

P(H|E) = [P(E|H) \* P(H)] / [P(E|H) \* P(H) + P(E|¬H) \* P(¬H)]

Substituting the given values, we get:

P(H|E) = (1.0 \* 0.15) / [(1.0 \* 0.15) + (0.2 \* 0.85)]
P(H|E) ≈ 0.4091 or 40.91%
